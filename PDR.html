Overview

This Preliminary Design Review (PDR) page documents the core problem and synthesized research context for HoverHaul—an on-site delivery concept to move small, critical supplies across dynamic construction environments.

Problem Statement

Construction workers lose time and face safety risks because critical small-scale supplies like water and tools are not delivered directly to them, causing dehydration, downtime, and costly project delays.

Problem Synthesis

Construction job sites face multiple overlapping problems that affect safety, efficiency, and overall productivity. One of the most pressing concerns is worker health, particularly hydration. Surveys show that many construction workers experience dehydration during shifts, which can lead to headaches, dizziness, and even serious health risks. Without regular water access, concentration drops and mistakes increase, creating safety hazards. The Hoover Dam construction project is a historical example that demonstrates the consequences of inadequate hydration, as extreme heat and poor water access endangered thousands of workers and slowed progress.

Inefficiency is another widespread problem. Studies reveal that the average construction worker spends about 38 hours each year simply searching for misplaced tools. Beyond this, companies lose valuable time and money when equipment is stolen or goes missing. Research shows that poor organization, unclear schedules, and wasted labor hours add up to significant project delays and higher costs. This lack of structure is compounded by the enormous amount of waste produced in construction. The EPA reports that construction and demolition debris make up a large portion of total U.S. waste, raising environmental and logistical issues. Effective site layout planning is critical, but many projects fail to prioritize it, resulting in job site chaos and inefficiencies.

Safety is the most critical challenge. The construction industry consistently records some of the highest workplace fatality rates. Falls, slips, and trips remain the leading cause of deaths and injuries, with OSHA, BLS, and legal studies all pointing to inadequate prevention strategies as a core issue.

Together, dehydration, wasted time, lost tools, poor layout planning, excessive waste, and high accident rates underscore the urgent need for innovative solutions.
